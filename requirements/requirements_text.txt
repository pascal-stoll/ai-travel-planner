1.  Requirement Report 
1.1  Introduction 
In the contemporary digital landscape, access to travel information has reached an unprecedented 
scale. Hundreds of booking platforms, travel blogs, review aggregators, social media channels, and 
destination guides compete simultaneously for the traveller's attention. Paradoxically, this abundance 
of information — rather than simplifying the planning process — has generated what behavioural 
economists describe as the "paradox of choice": the more options are available, the greater the 
cognitive burden experienced by the decision-maker, and the lower the resulting satisfaction. 
 
Planning a trip has effectively become a second, unpaid job . A traveller seeking a long weekend 
away must typically: cross -reference flight aggregators with hotel booking platforms, reconcile 
contradictory TripAdvisor reviews with Instagram posts, manually verify opening hours and seasonal 
closures, estimate travel times between attractions, and finally synthesise all of this into a coherent, 
logistically viable schedule — all before the journey has even begun. Research by the Google Travel 
Insights division consistently shows that travellers visit an average of 38 different websites in the 45 
days preceding a booking decision. This fragmentation of information sources produces decision 
fatigue: a well-documented psychological phenomenon whereby the quality of decisions deteriorates 
sharply after prolonged exposure to complex choices. 
 
The consequences of this inefficiency extend beyond mere inconvenience. Decision fatigue in travel 
planning manifests as: the tendency to default to familiar, "safe" destinations rather than exploring 
alternatives genuinely aligned with personal preference s; the abandonment of planning efforts 
entirely, resulting in suboptimal last -minute bookings; and post -trip dissatisfaction stemming from 
itineraries that prioritised logistical convenience over experiential quality. 
 
Despite significant investment from the travel technology sector, existing digital solutions have failed 
to resolve this structural problem. Current tools — whether booking engines, itinerary builders, or 
recommendation platforms — operate on a paradigm of  rigid, filter-based data queries that require 
the user to already know, with precision, what they want. They demand structured inputs (dates, 
destination, budget range, star rating) at a stage when the user's mental state is inherently unstructured: 
a mood, a feeling, a vague aspiration. The fundamental mismatch between the fluidity of human 
desire and the rigidity of current interfaces constitutes the core problem that TravelMind is designed 
to address.

1.2  Background 
1.2.1  The Failure of Rigid Filter-Based Systems 
Contemporary travel platforms are architected around a structured-query model: the user is required 
to specify discrete, machine -readable parameters — origin, destination, departure date, return date, 
number of passengers, budget ceiling — before any resul ts are surfaced. This model was designed 
for an era when computational resources were scarce and search indexes were shallow. It assumes 
that the user possesses, at the outset of their planning process, a level of decisional clarity that, in 
practice, very few travellers have. 
 
Human travel motivations are inherently psychographic in nature. A traveller does not begin their 
planning journey thinking "I want to go to Lisbon on the 14th of June for three nights with a budget 
of €600". They begin with an emotional state: "I need to disconnect from work", "I want to eat 
incredible food in an authentic setting", "I am looking for something adventurous but not physically 
demanding". These psychographic signals — mood, aspiration, cognitive need — are entirely 
invisible to filter-based systems, which cannot process them.

The consequences are systematic: users are forced to translate their fluid, emotional intent into the 
rigid syntax of a search form, losing critical nuance in the process. The resulting recommendations 
are optimised for logistical parameters (price, availa bility, distance) rather than for experiential fit. 
The user receives technically correct results that are experientially misaligned — a failure mode that 
current platforms have no mechanism to detect or correct.

1.2.2  Natural Language Processing as the Enabling Technology 
The technological precondition for TravelMind's approach is the maturation of large -scale Natural 
Language Processing (NLP) models, specifically Large Language Models (LLMs) trained on broad 
corpora of human-generated text. These models have demonstrated a remarkable capacity to perform 
semantic interpretation: the ability to extract structured meaning — intent, sentiment, constraint, 
preference — from unstructured, colloquial, and even grammatically imperfect natural language 
input. 
 
Consider the following user prompt: "Voglio qualcosa di rilassante, lontano dalla folla, ma con 
buon cibo e non troppo caro" . A filter -based system receives this as null input — no parseable 
parameters. An NLP -powered system, by contrast, extracts a rich semantic payload: a low-
stimulation environment (relaxing), a preference for non-touristic areas (away from crowds), a 
high weighting on gastronomic quality  (good food), and a budget sensitivity signal  (not too 
expensive). From these four semantic anchors, TravelMind can generate a destination shortlist, filter 
it against geospatial and logistical constraints, and produce a fully structured itinerary — all within 
seconds. 
 
The power of this approach lies in its capacity for contextual inference: the ability to fill in unstated 
but contextually implied constraints. A user travelling with young children does not need to 
enumerate every child -safety requirement; the system infe rs them from the family context. A user 
requesting an "adventurous" itinerary in winter does not need to exclude ski resorts; the system infers 
geographic and seasonal compatibility automatically. This inference layer radically reduces the 
cognitive burden on the user.

🔬  Technical Note: Prompt Engineering as Core IP 
The quality of TravelMind's output is directly proportional to the quality of its prompt 
engineering layer. The system translates structured user inputs (collected via the wizard UI) 
into a semantically rich, constraint -encoded prompt that is submitted to the LLM API. This 
prompt engineering module — specifying output schema (JSON), constraint hierarchy, tone, 
and domain vocabulary — constitutes the primary technical differentiator of the TravelMind 
system and is therefore the most critical component of the AI Wrapper layer. 
 
1.2.3  AI as Expert Curator: From Big Data to Tailored Recommendations 
Beyond semantic interpretation, the second key value driver of an AI -powered travel planner is its 
capacity for large -scale data aggregation and synthesis. A human travel expert — a high -end 
concierge, a destination specialist — derives their value from years of accumulated knowledge about 
places, experiences, logistics, and seasonal nuances. They can recall that a particular coastal town is 
best visited in shoulder season, that a specific restaurant requires booking six weeks in advance, or 
that a cultural  museum's permanent collection is undergoing renovation. This accumulated, cross -
referenced expertise is what transforms a list of places into a genuinely useful itinerary. 
 
An LLM trained on a sufficiently broad corpus of travel literature, reviews, geographic data, and 
cultural information can approximate this curatorial expertise at scale, instantaneously, and without 
the economic barrier of a human specialist. TravelMind l everages this capability to act as a 
democratised expert curator: providing every user, regardless of budget or prior travel knowledge, 
with the quality of recommendation previously available only to luxury travellers with access to 
specialist concierge services. 
 
Critically, the AI's curatorial function extends beyond simple recommendation to include logistical 
coherence: the generated itinerary respects opening hours, travel times between stops, meal timing, 
cognitive pacing (avoiding back -to-back high-stimulation activities), and budget distribution across 
the trip duration. This multi -constraint optimisation — trivial for an AI system, enormously time -
consuming for a human planner — is the central efficiency gain that TravelMind delivers. 
 
1.3  Purpose 
The primary goal of the TravelMind project, developed as part of the Software Engineering 
programme, is to design, implement, and validate a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) of an AI -
powered travel planning web application. The MVP is scoped to demonstrate the  core value 
proposition of the system — the translation of unstructured user preferences into a structured, 
actionable travel itinerary — while maintaining implementational feasibility within the project 
timeline.

1.3.2  Reduction of Time-to-Plan as the Primary KPI 
The central, measurable outcome of TravelMind is the reduction of Time-to-Plan: the elapsed time 
from the user's initial intent to plan a trip to the moment they possess a complete, actionable itinerary. 
Based on the baseline data presented in Section 3.1.1 (4 –6 hours of manual planning for a leisure 

--- PAGE 6 ---
trip), TravelMind targets a Time-to-Plan of under 5 minutes for the core wizard-to-itinerary flow, 
representing a reduction of approximately 98% relative to the current manual baseline. 
 
This target is achievable through three compounding efficiency mechanisms: (1) the wizard interface 
eliminates the need to visit multiple external platforms; (2) the AI layer performs destination research 
and logistics planning in parallel, not sequentially; and (3) the structured JSON output schema ensures 
zero latency between AI response and frontend rendering, with no manual reformatting required by 
the user. 
 
 MVP Success Criteria Summary 
The TravelMind MVP will be considered successful if it demonstrates: (1) end -to-end flow 
from wizard input to rendered itinerary in under 5 minutes; (2) semantic accuracy — AI-
generated destinations are rated as 'relevant' or 'highly relevant' by at least 80% of test users 
given their stated input; (3) zero unhandled API errors in the demo scenario — all failure 
modes produce graceful, user-actionable error states; and (4) full mobile responsiveness across 
standard viewport widths (360 px – 1440 px). 
 
1.3.3  Academic Scope and Learning Objectives 
In addition to its functional goals, the TravelMind project is designed to provide the development 
team with structured, hands-on experience across the core competencies of the Software Engineering 
curriculum: 
 
• Requirements Engineering: Functional and non-functional requirements elicitation, user story 
mapping, and use case modelling. 
• AI Integration and Prompt Engineering: Design of structured LLM prompts, handling of stochastic API 
responses, and implementation of JSON schema enforcement for reliable output parsing.  
• System Architecture: Separation of concerns across frontend (React/Vanilla JS), AI wrapper 
(Node.js/Express), and data persistence (localStorage) layers. 
• Project Management: Work Breakdown Structure (WBS), Critical Path Method (CPM), and sprint -based 
iterative delivery. 
• Testing and Quality Assurance: API error handling validation, responsive design testing, and user 
acceptance testing (UAT) against defined success criteria.  
 
 TravelMind does not aim to replace the joy of travel discovery — it aims to eliminate the 
friction that prevents that discovery from happening. By positioning AI not as a replacement 
for human judgment but as an intelligent pre -processing layer that organi ses the world's 
travel information around the individual user's actual desires, TravelMind represents a 
structurally new paradigm in travel planning software.

UC-06 is selected as the primary narrative because it exercises all three system layers (frontend, AI

of TravelMind. It is the convergence point for UC-01, UC-03, and UC-05.

Main Flow (Happy Path) 
1. User completes preference input via either the three-button fast-path (UC-01) or the extended wizard (UC-
02), with optional destination selection (UC-05) for the extended path. 
2. System constructs a semantically structured prompt encoding: travel style, departure city/GPS, max distance, 
duration, budget tier (default Mid-Range for fast-path), group composition (default 2 adults/0 children), and 
transport mode (default Car + Public Transport). [RF-AI01] 
3. System validates the constructed prompt against the predefined JSON schema template. 
4. System submits prompt to DeepSeek LLM API via the Node.js/Express backend proxy. 
5. Loading Screen is displayed: animated compass icon, rotating status messages (2.5 s cycle), three coloured 
Mood/Duration/Radius chips, indeterminate progress bar. [RF-LD01] 
6. AI API processes the request and returns a JSON response. 
7. System validates the API response against the predefined JSON schema. [RF-AI08] 
8. System parses validated JSON and populates the frontend data model. 
9. System auto-saves: serialises complete itinerary JSON to localStorage under UUID-v4 key with destination 
name, timestamp, trip type, duration tier, and full payload. [RF-STO01] 
10. System renders Results Screen: full-bleed destination photo (RF-VIS04), day-card list view default (RF-VIS01), 
map view toggle (RF-VIS02, RF-VIS03).

3.5 DFD (DATA FLOW DIAGRAM)  
DFD — Subgroup 1: Preference Input & Configuration 
What this subgroup does Collects user travel preferences and constructs the structured prompt for 
the AI. It never calls the API directly — its only output is a validated parameter object passed to 
Subgroup 2. 
Three entry points, one output The user can enter in three ways: Fast-Path (3 sequential taps 
Mood → Duration → Radius), Extended Wizard (6-step form), or Surprise Me (single tap, full 
randomisation). All three converge on the same validation process. 
How data flows The user feeds P1, P2, or P3 depending on the chosen path. GPS input enters only 
through P1 to resolve the departure city in the Radius selector. P1 reads and writes localStorage to 
persist selections for 24h. P2 and P3 write to an in-memory buffer. All three input processes feed 
into P4, which validates the parameters and injects missing defaults for the fast-path (Budget = 
Mid-Range, Group = 2 adults / 0 children, Transport = Car + Public Transport). P4 passes the clean 
parameters to P5, which encodes the final JSON prompt and forwards it to Subgroup 2. P5 also 
writes the 3 summary chips back to localStorage for display on the loading screen. 
Two data stores DS-SG1-001 is the localStorage store with a 24h TTL — it holds the fast-path 
selections and the summary chips. DS-SG1-002 is an in-memory buffer — it exists only for the 
duration of the wizard session and is destroyed after prompt construction completes. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 


--- PAGE 32 ---
Subgroup 2: AI Generation Pipeline 
 
 
 
What it does Receives the structured prompt from Subgroup 1, manages all communication with 
the DeepSeek API, and delivers a validated, persisted itinerary JSON to Subgroup 3 for rendering. 
Two paths through the same pipeline On the fast-path, one API call is made and the AI returns a 
full itinerary directly. On the extended wizard path, two calls are made: the first returns 2–3 
destination suggestions which the user selects from (UC-04/05), the second generates the full 
itinerary for the chosen destination (UC-06). Both paths exit through the same P4 process. 
How data flows P1 fires the API request and simultaneously renders the loading screen with the 3 
summary chips. The raw response hits P2, which validates it against the predefined JSON schema. 
If validation fails, P2 triggers one automatic retry back to P1 — if that also fails, the user sees an 
error state with Retry and Edit options. On success, validated data is written to the in-memory 
request context (DS-SG2-001). P3 is only active on the extended path: it surfaces destination cards 
to the user and, on selection, fires a second request back through P1. P4 reads the final itinerary 
from DS-SG2-001, serialises it to localStorage under a UUID-v4 key, and both the raw JSON and 
the save confirmation flow downstream to Subgroup 3. 
Two data stores DS-SG2-001 is an in-memory context buffer holding the current API response — 
cleared after the itinerary is forwarded. DS-SG2-002 is the localStorage itinerary store — persistent, 
survives refresh, cleared only by explicit user action in Subgroup 4. 
Mermaid DFD — Subgroup 3: Results Screen Interaction 
 
 
How Subgroup 3 works 


--- PAGE 33 ---
What it does Receives the validated itinerary from Subgroup 2 and handles everything the user 
sees and interacts with on the results screen — viewing, modifying individual stops, and swapping 
the destination entirely. 
How data flows The itinerary JSON from Subgroup 2 lands in both DS-SG3-001 (the active state 
store) and P1 simultaneously. P1 renders the results screen: destination header with photo from 
Unsplash (with a local placeholder fallback if the API is unavailable), and the day-card list view as 
default. The user can toggle to map view, which routes the itinerary data through P2 — Leaflet.js 
renders numbered markers and a transport-adaptive polyline; tapping a marker opens a stop detail 
bottom sheet. If the user taps the refresh icon on a single stop, P3 reads the surrounding stops from 
DS-SG3-001 for context, fires a targeted re-request to DeepSeek for that stop only, and merges the 
result back into the active state without touching the rest of the itinerary. If the user taps "Different 
destination?" (fast-path only), P4 reads the original prompt parameters from DS-SG3-001, re-
submits the full prompt with an explicit exclusion instruction for the current destination, and on 
response replaces the entire active itinerary and triggers P1 to re-render. The active itinerary in DS-
SG3-001 is always the single source of truth — any modification by P3 or P4 goes through the store 
first. 
One data store DS-SG3-001 is the in-memory active itinerary state. It is populated by Subgroup 2 
on first load, updated in-place by P3 (stop-level) or P4 (full replace), and read by Subgroup 4 for 
export and sharing operations. 
 
Mermaid DFD — Subgroup 4: Itinerary Management & Distribution 
 
 
How Subgroup 4 works 
What it does Manages the post-generation lifecycle of itineraries — browsing and deleting saved 
trips, exporting to PDF, and generating shareable links. This subgroup never calls the AI API and 
never modifies itinerary content. 
How data flows DS-SG4-001 is the central localStorage trip history store, written to by Subgroup 2 
automatically on every successful generation. P1 reads from it to populate the My Trips screen, 
applying a real-time name filter if the user types a search query — an empty state illustration is 
shown when no results match. P2 handles deletion: it shows a confirmation modal before removing 
any entry from localStorage, then triggers P1 to refresh the list. P3 and P4 both read the active 


--- PAGE 34 ---
itinerary directly from Subgroup 3 — neither touches the history store. P3 passes the formatted 
itinerary to the jsPDF library client-side (no server required) and delivers the PDF blob as a browser 
download. P4 Base64-encodes the full itinerary JSON into a URL query parameter and writes it to 
the Clipboard API, which triggers a transient confirmation toast to the user. 
One data store DS-SG4-001 is the persistent localStorage trip history. It is the only store in this 
subgroup — written by Subgroup 2, read and modified by P1 and P2. P3 and P4 operate entirely 
from the in-memory active itinerary passed in from Subgroup 3 and produce no persistent side 
effects. 
 
 
 
How the unified DFD works 
Overall flow The system is a strict left-to-right pipeline. Subgroup 1 collects and validates user 
preferences and hands off a structured JSON prompt. Subgroup 2 fires the AI request, validates the 
response, and produces a persisted itinerary. Subgroup 3 renders and lets the user modify the 
results. Subgroup 4 manages the saved history and handles export and sharing. Each subgroup has a 
clean input boundary and a clean output boundary — no subgroup reaches back into a prior one. 
Inter-subgroup connections There are five cross-boundary flows. SG1→SG2 is the structured 
prompt string. SG2→SG3 is the validated itinerary JSON flowing both into the render process and 
directly into the active state store. SG2→SG4 is the auto-save write to the trip history localStorage 
store, which happens in parallel with the render. SG3→SG4 are two read flows — the active in-
memory itinerary is read by the PDF export and share link processes without touching the history 
store. 
API is called from two subgroups SG2 owns the primary generation calls. SG3 calls api 
independently for single-stop regeneration and destination swap — these are partial re-requests that 
bypass SG2 entirely and write their results directly back into the active itinerary state.
